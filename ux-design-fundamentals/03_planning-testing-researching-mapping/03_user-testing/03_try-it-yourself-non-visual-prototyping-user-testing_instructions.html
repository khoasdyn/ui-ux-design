Now that I've discussed non-visual prototyping and user testing with you, I'd like you to try it out by developing a paper prototype and testing it out on a user yourself. This is a useful exercise to observe user patterns and behavior, and understand where points of confusion may exist for your user. There's no set way to do this—the best approach is allow the user to make their own decisions as much as possible and allow them to talk through their decisions so you can observe the results.

  1. If you haven't already, develop an interaction map for your app as it will help you develop your paper prototype. Once you've made your map, use it as a reference to guide you in "dismantling" your app onto single sheets of paper.

  2. On another sheet of paper, write down the tasks that users should be able to complete in your prototype. If there is a specific order to the tasks, please note that too.

  3. Recruit a friend or family member to be your test user. Don't worry about the person actually being from your app's target user group, as the point of this activity is to give you some experience with this method of user testing. Of course, if you can find someone who meets the profile of your target group, all the better!

  4. Find a comfortable spot for the test, such as at a table or desk. Seat yourself across from your test user, or beside them. Have your paper "screens" next to you, face down, in a little pile, along with your list of tasks, the list of questions below, and a notepad and pencil for taking notes.

  5. Explain to the user the purpose of the test: you're testing a prototype for an app and you want to observe their thoughts and reactions as they move through the various functions of the prototype. Explain that you will be asking them some questions throughout the process. Encourage the test user to speak out loud as they evaluate each screen of the app and make decisions throughout the test.

  6. Hand the test user one "screen" from your stack and ask them a question or two from the list below. Listen to their answer. Take notes. Observe how the user interacts with the screen before handing them another piece of paper from your stack.

Testing Questions (not an exhaustive list)

  • Before even looking at the prototype, what do you expect to do with this app?

  • What is the first thing you notice on this screen?

  • What is the first thing you would do on this screen?

  • Your task is X. What would be the first thing you would do here to accomplish that task?

  • (after observing the user press a particular part of the interface) Why did you choose that button?

  • Is there anything on this screen that seems out of place?

  • Is there anything missing from this screen?

  • Is the prototype easy to use?

  • How do you feel when you are using the prototype?

  • What would you change about the prototype?
