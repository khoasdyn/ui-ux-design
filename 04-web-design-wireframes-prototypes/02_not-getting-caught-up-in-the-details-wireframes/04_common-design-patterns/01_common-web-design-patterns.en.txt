An accordion is a vertically stacked list of items, each of which can be expanded to reveal more contents. This design pattern is useful for organizing information in a compact way, allowing users to easily navigate through sections without being overwhelmed by too much content at once. A good use case for accordions is a list of frequently asked questions. On the Order website, we can see such a list on the Become a Member page. Another design pattern that organizes information in a compact way are tabs. They are related to accordions. There is a heading that can be clicked to reveal more information. Tabs though work best with very short headings, so they wouldn't be great for an FAQ list, for example. Also with tabs, only one of them can be visible at a time. Lastly, I want to mention a design pattern that can create dynamic page layouts, especially for editorial content. I call it 25-50-25, which refers to the column sizes of the page. There are three columns. The first and last columns are 25% in width, and the middle column is 50%. This simple grid allows for pretty interesting layout decisions for images, text, captions, pull quotes, etc. The main text will always go into the center column. Images, captions, and pull quotes can also live in the outside columns. Images can even span multiple columns. Here is an example in the wild. Let's look at a really nicely designed online magazine called The Great Discontent. They publish a lot of interviews with creative people. As you can see, the layout starts with a large image of the interview subject followed by a headline and an introduction. Then in the lighter area, the 25-50-25 emerges. There are three columns with the center column occupying about 50% of the space flanked by smaller columns on either side. The main text flows down the center, while the left and right columns are filled with secondary content, such as images and pull quotes. When scrolling down, we see that one image spans both the left and the center columns, with the right column containing a pull quote. And here are three images spanning all three columns from left to right. What I appreciate about this design is its ability to structure large amounts of text and images in a way that remains engaging and visually interesting for the reader.
