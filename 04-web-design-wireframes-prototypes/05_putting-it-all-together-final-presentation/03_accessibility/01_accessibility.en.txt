Let's take a moment to discuss how focusing on accessibility allows you to create more usable websites for everyone. This is not meant to be a deep dive into accessibility, it's really too vast a field, but I will give you tips that you can implement right away in your website designs. Accessibility means empowering people of all abilities. It's ensuring that everyone, not just some people, can enjoy and interact with the products you design. People may have impairments that fall into four general categories, visual, hearing, physical, and cognitive. Under visual impairments, you might encounter individuals who are blind, have low vision, or have a hard time differentiating between colors. Hearing impairments include those who are completely deaf and those who are hard of hearing, perhaps due to an accident or old age. For physical impairments, some individuals might have lost limbs, making it difficult for them to use digital devices in traditional ways. Others may have mobility issues or limited fine motor control, which can make tasks like using a mouse or trackpad challenging. Finally, under cognitive impairments, there are individuals with dyslexia, attention disorders, or dementia. Now, here's something important to remember. None of us are permanently free from these limitations. You can even say that we are all only ever temporarily not disabled. Let me explain what I mean with a few examples. Think about someone who has lost an arm. They can't hold a phone in one hand and type with the other. When designing a mobile phone operating system, let's say, designers need to consider how such a limitation might affect the user experience. But guess what? The same limitation applies to a parent holding a child in one arm. They also only have one hand free in this case. Here's another example. Someone with low vision would benefit from text that has enough color contrast. But so would someone with perfect vision standing on a beach in bright sunlight struggling with glare on their phone screen. Proper color contrast isn't just for those with impairments. It helps everyone. And one last example. A deaf person watching a video needs subtitles to understand the content. But subtitles are also helpful for someone in a noisy environment like a subway, where it's hard to hear the audio. Or the exact opposite. Someone in a quiet space like a library. If they forgot their headphones, they will appreciate being able to read the subtitles. So accessibility isn't just about edge cases or a small group of people. Designing with accessibility in mind benefits everyone. It makes websites easier to use for all of us because, again, we are all only ever temporarily not disabled. Now let's go over some common accessibility considerations. These can be grouped into four categories. Coding, content, design, and writing. Under coding, it's crucial to write semantic HTML. This means using the most descriptive tags for your content and using headline tags such as h1 and h2 in the proper order. Also, keyboard navigation should be possible for users who are unable to use a mouse. For content, make sure videos have closed captions and that images have descriptive alt text so screen readers can convey their meaning to visually impaired users. Under design, two aspects make a big difference. Type size and color contrast. And lastly, avoid using the link label. Click here. Let's take a closer look at these last three. A good rule of thumb is that body text should be at least 16 pixels. Some text like captions or image credits can go a bit smaller, but paragraph text should never dip below 16 pixels. And bigger screens need even bigger type. Why? Well, consider how far your smartphone is from your eyes when you hold it in your hands. A lot closer than the screen of your laptop, right? So on larger screens, increase your type size to 20 or even 24 pixels. Color contrast refers to how much text stands out against its background. For large text over 19 pixels, the contrast ratio should be at least 3 to 1. For small text, it should be even higher at 4.5 to 1. You can check these ratios using online tools like AIM Contrast Checker or the A11y plugin in Figma. I'll mention here that it's fine for most projects to strive for AA compliance. There are more stringent AAA rules too, but again, AA should be sufficient. And finally, let's talk about using click here for link text. Avoid it. It's one of the most common accessibility mistakes. Let me give you an example. Here's an event page from the Pasadena Conservatory of Music website. Let's look closely at the text. Click here is used four times. What's the problem? Well, try to find out quickly what each link does. You can't, because they all say the same thing. It's hard to scan this text. All you see is here, here, here, here. And you have to look for the text after each link to figure out what each link does. And that's quite taxing for a human brain. Or it's at least much more difficult than having more meaningful link text in the first place. So let's rewrite this one by one. The button will change to watch on zoom. That's much more precise.
