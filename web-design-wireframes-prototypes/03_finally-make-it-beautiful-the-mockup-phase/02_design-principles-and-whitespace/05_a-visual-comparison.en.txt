I'll quickly talk about two more real-world examples that nicely illustrate how white space makes a big difference in how we experience visual design. We'll look at two websites from colleges in the Los Angeles area. One of the sites pretty bad and one of them pretty good. The first one from Pierce College illustrates the problems of not considering white space enough. There's too much content at once and not enough space between elements. Our eyes don't know where to focus first here and the overall feeling is one of chaos and claustrophobia. That by the way doesn't mean that the information architecture here is necessarily bad or that the content is bad or even that the college is bad. The site is simply hard to look at and the poor design makes the user feel lost and most of all it doesn't look like it will be a fun experience to browse the site and in the end what does that say about attending this particular college. Sure, it's only a first impression but fair or not, first impressions matter and will influence a user's perception and ultimately the success of a sites. The other site is from El Camino College and while it's not the pinnacle of design excellence. In comparison the site feels like a refuge, while the previous site had more than three dozen options presented all at once, here we only have a handful. There's a sense of refinement, and clarity, and sanity, and we can simply scroll down to find a lot of other options. The generous use of white space here really helps us to make sense of the information.
